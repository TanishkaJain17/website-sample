A Space for Real Questions

Ask Sundeep

Every question carries a story. Every story reveals a pattern waiting to be recognised.

Share the question that has quietly been following you. It may be about relationships, purpose, ambition, identity, grief, leadership, conflict, love, family, or simply a feeling you have never been able to name.

Mythology may not give you an answer. But it may help you see your question differently.

Ask Your Question

Why Ask?

Life rarely gives us completely new problems. Across thousands of years, human beings have wrestled with remarkably similar questions — in the Mahabharata, the Ramayana, the Puranas, Greek myths, Norse legends. Every culture has attempted to understand the same human patterns.

Sometimes what feels like your personal struggle is actually an ancient pattern waiting to be recognised. That is where mythology becomes useful — not as religion, not as history, but as a language for understanding life.

"Once you understand that mythology is psychology, you can never un-see it."

Philosophy

Core Propositions

I.

Mythology is Psychology

Every mythic character is an archetype — a crystallised pattern of human behaviour. To understand Karna is to understand ambition and wounds. To understand Draupadi is to understand dignity under injustice.

II.

Stories are Mirrors

A myth doesn't tell you what to think. It holds up a mirror and asks you to look honestly. The stories that disturb you most are often the ones most useful to you.

III.

Curiosity Before Belief

Mythology is not a faith system. It is a thinking system. The moment you approach it with curiosity rather than belief, it becomes infinitely more powerful.

IV.

Ancient. Timeless. Now.

The Ramayana, the Mahabharata, the Puranas — they were not written for another age. They were written for this moment, for you, in your specific struggle.

About the Book

Mythology · Poetry · Feminine Archetypes

Ahalya is one of the Panchakanya — the five sacred women of Indian mythology. Traditionally remembered for a curse, Sundeep Verma's retelling asks a different question: what if she wasn't a victim of another's story, but the author of her own desire?

Written in flowing, sensory verse, the book moves through longing, transgression, silence, and emergence. Each illustration is not decoration — it is interpretation. Together, text and image make a case for reading ancient women on their own terms.

An ancient symbol for the contemporary woman's conflict between her mind and her heart.
