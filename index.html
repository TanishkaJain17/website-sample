A Space for Real Questions

Ask Sundeep

Every question carries a story. Every story reveals a pattern waiting to be recognised.

Share the question that has quietly been following you. It may be about relationships, purpose, ambition, identity, grief, leadership, conflict, love, family, or simply a feeling you have never been able to name.

Mythology may not give you an answer. But it may help you see your question differently.

Ask Your Question

Why Ask?

Life rarely gives us completely new problems. Across thousands of years, human beings have wrestled with remarkably similar questions — in the Mahabharata, the Ramayana, the Puranas, Greek myths, Norse legends. Every culture has attempted to understand the same human patterns.

Sometimes what feels like your personal struggle is actually an ancient pattern waiting to be recognised. That is where mythology becomes useful — not as religion, not as history, but as a language for understanding life.

A journey through stories

Early Career

Advertising & Brand Strategy

Leadership roles at Ogilvy, Grey, and Omnicom Group — building brand narratives for some of India's largest organisations. Learning that all communication is, at its core, storytelling.

"I was always telling stories. I just hadn't found the oldest ones yet."

London

Central Saint Martins, UAL

Trained in Narrative Environment Design — understanding how space, story, and human experience intersect. The seed of the Mythology Lab was planted here.

The Turn

From Brand Stories to Mythic Ones

Leaving the corporate world behind, Sundeep turned to the oldest stories humanity ever told — asking why they still echo inside us today. The workshops began in intimate circles.

"Once you understand that mythology is psychology, you can never un-see it."

2021

Ahalya — The Unploughed

Published his debut book — a visceral poetic retelling of one of Indian mythology's most misread women. Received as a work that "reads like a reckoning."

Present

Cobalt Blue Foundation

Founded to build mythology as a discipline for self-inquiry, cultural leadership, and institutional storytelling. 150+ workshops. 13+ cultural institutions. Still growing.

Philosophy

Core Propositions

I.

Mythology is Psychology

Every mythic character is an archetype — a crystallised pattern of human behaviour. To understand Karna is to understand ambition and wounds. To understand Draupadi is to understand dignity under injustice.

II.

Stories are Mirrors

A myth doesn't tell you what to think. It holds up a mirror and asks you to look honestly. The stories that disturb you most are often the ones most useful to you.

III.

Curiosity Before Belief

Mythology is not a faith system. It is a thinking system. The moment you approach it with curiosity rather than belief, it becomes infinitely more powerful.

IV.

Ancient. Timeless. Now.

The Ramayana, the Mahabharata, the Puranas — they were not written for another age. They were written for this moment, for you, in your specific struggle.

Sundeep's sessions are not informational. They are experiential. Every workshop is built around a specific human question — and mythology becomes the language through which participants explore their own answers.

The mythology is not the destination. The participant is.

About the Book

Mythology · Poetry · Feminine Archetypes

Ahalya is one of the Panchakanya — the five sacred women of Indian mythology. Traditionally remembered for a curse, Sundeep Verma's retelling asks a different question: what if she wasn't a victim of another's story, but the author of her own desire?

Written in flowing, sensory verse, the book moves through longing, transgression, silence, and emergence. Each illustration is not decoration — it is interpretation. Together, text and image make a case for reading ancient women on their own terms.

An ancient symbol for the contemporary woman's conflict between her mind and her heart.

When Gods Came Home

An immersive mythology experience where ancient deities are encountered not in temples, but in everyday life — homes, kitchens, conversations, and crossroads. A living installation of myth and meaning.

VenueCobalt Blue Foundation, Delhi NCR
DatesOngoing · Seasonal Editions
FormatImmersive Installation

The Program

A myth you can walk through

When Gods Came Home reimagines mythology as lived experience rather than distant history. Visitors move through a sequence of everyday spaces — a kitchen, a doorway, a courtyard, a crossroads — each quietly inhabited by a deity or archetype, encountered the way you might encounter a memory: unannounced, and unmistakably familiar.

The installation was conceived by Sundeep Verma and produced by the Cobalt Blue Foundation as a way of asking a simple question in a physical form: what if the gods never left — what if we simply stopped noticing them in the ordinary rooms of our lives?

Event Highlights

01

Immersive Rooms

Five sequential spaces, each staged as a myth made domestic and physical.

02

Live Storytelling

Guided walkthroughs with Sundeep Verma, blending performance and reflection.

03

Reflection Room

A closing space for visitors to write the question the experience left them with.

The Studio · By Ancient Life

Meet the character whose story continues to live today.

Six lives, six lessons that never really stopped being useful.

Ar

Arjuna

The Seeker

The greatest archer in the Mahabharata is remembered for the moment he couldn't lift his bow. His paralysis on the battlefield of Kurukshetra is the paralysis of someone who has outgrown the story they were given to live.

Where are you waiting for certainty before allowing yourself to begin?

K

Karna

The Wounded Hero

Gifted, denied, and loyal anyway. Karna spent his entire life on the outside — his tragedy was never his defeat, but the wound of unbelonging that shaped every choice he made.

Where do you still feel like an outsider, even in rooms you've earned?

Ek

Eklavya

The Devoted Practitioner

Refused a teacher, Eklavya built one from clay and practiced before it until he surpassed the students who were chosen. His mastery asks nothing of permission — only of repetition, alone, unseen.

What would you practice if no one was ever going to applaud it?

D

Draupadi

The Sovereign

Humiliated in a court built to break her, Draupadi didn't weep or beg. She asked a question no one could answer — and that question became the seed of a war, and the beginning of her power.

What injustice have you gone quiet around that still deserves a question?

Ah

Ahalya

The Unploughed

Remembered for a curse, misread for centuries as a warning. Her story is not about forgiveness — it is about what happens when the world decides a woman's desire was never hers to have, and what it costs everyone when she takes it back.

What version of yourself deserves another beginning?

H

Hanuman

The Devoted

Beloved not for his power but for his devotion. His strength appears the exact moment he forgets himself entirely in service of something he loves completely.

What do you do best only when you've stopped thinking about yourself?

The Studio · The Ritual Cabinet

Ancient practices for modern questions.

Not products. Practices. Open the one that matches where you are right now.

Begin

Inspired by Arjuna

A morning ritual for uncertain beginnings.

+
Story

Every beginning asks for courage before it offers certainty. Arjuna's bow did not become lighter — his hands simply learned to lift it anyway.

Practice

Each morning, write one sentence naming the thing you're avoiding beginning. Read it once. Fold it. Begin anyway.

Companion Object

A Beginning Journal — one line a day, no pressure to fill the page.

Practice

Inspired by Eklavya

A daily ritual for devotion without recognition.

+
Story

Eklavya practiced before a teacher made of clay until he surpassed the students who were chosen. Mastery never asked for an audience.

Practice

Choose one small skill. Practice it for ten unwitnessed minutes daily, for one week, without telling anyone.

Companion Object

A Practice Notebook with a simple habit tracker — one mark, no explanation needed.

Let Go

Inspired by Shiva

A ritual for releasing what no longer belongs.

+
Story

Shiva does not destroy from anger. He destroys from necessity — because some things must end before something true can begin.

Practice

Write down what you are holding that has finished its purpose. Read it aloud once. Then let the page go — recycle it, burn it, put it away.

Companion Object

A set of Reflection Cards, one for each thing you're ready to release.

Find Your Voice

Inspired by Draupadi

A ritual for speaking with courage.

+
Story

Draupadi's power began with a single unanswerable question, asked plainly, in a room built to silence her.

Practice

Name one question you have been too polite to ask. Write it exactly as you'd say it out loud. Keep it somewhere you'll see it.

Companion Object

A Conversation Deck of questions worth asking, even when your voice shakes.

Begin Again

Inspired by Ahalya

A ritual for renewal.

+
Story

Ahalya's story does not end with the stone. It ends with the quiet, unhurried relief of being seen again as more than a warning.

Practice

Each new moon, write one line describing who you are becoming — not who you were. Keep the old lines. Don't erase them.

Companion Object

An Awakening Candle, lit only on the days you choose to start again.

Remember Your Worth

Inspired by Karna

A ritual for separating identity from recognition.

+
Story

Karna's worth was never decided by the court that denied him. His story asks what remains of a person once the applause is subtracted.

Practice

List three things you value about yourself that no one has ever praised. Read the list before you check your phone in the morning.

Companion Object

A Worth Journal, kept private, read only by you.
